Simple Health Information System

Simple Health Information System

IVR systems have several advantages over existing information disseminating methods. IVR systems are typically cheaper to deploy and scale. These systems are also interactive and can be easily managed by health organizations, such as Alight. IVR systems provide greater accessibility -- particularly for low literacy populations. Part of our project will be to remotely test and train participants to use the IVR system during COVID-19.

How does SHIS Work?

Refugees can use either a tactile or smartphone to dial a designated phone number to use the SHIS. After initiating the call, a voice recording will provide an introduction and instructions on how to use the system. Users will then be prompted with several questions. The answers to these questions will be used to curate information specific to their needs.

Our Objective

The objective of SHIS is to assist NGOs with their ongoing pandemic response and to provide refugees in Mahama Camp with supplemental access to COVID-19 information provided by the Ministry of Health in Rwanda.  

Phases of our Project

The SHIS project is separated into three iterative phases: 1) co-design, 2) training, and 3) deployment.  

Co-Design

In phase 1 (Co-Design), we address: “How can voice-based interfaces be designed for users in low-resource contexts to interact with health systems in Rwanda?” To design a system for the refugees, we will work with the Alight clients (30 refugees) by observing them remotely (through conference calls) as they navigate an early version of the IVR system. The user’s goal will be to complete a series of tasks on the IVR by pressing buttons or speaking on their phones. After they finish the task, we will immediately ask to follow up survey questions verbally over the phone based on their usage of the IVR. We expect to iteratively update the IVR based on user feedback.

Training

In phase 2 (Training), we address: “What training intervention will make access to IVR systems in Rwanda more effective?” Training users or onboarding is useful when users have little or no prior exposure to using IVR systems, however training can cause a bottleneck towards scaling. So, we will test the system in two training conditions i.e. no training and with training with 25 users each. The rest of this phase will be similar to phase 1 where users use the system and then answer a survey.

Deployment

In phase 3 (Deployment), we address : “How do refugees use IVR systems in Rwanda to access dynamic and locally relevant health-related information?” In the context of COVID-19, we will use pre-approved messages curated by Alight staff and well suited to the camp. We will learn from prior phases and redesign the IVR and aim for a small scale deployment of 150 refugees. The number will be broadcast in the Mahama camp and the participants would be able to access the system using the toll-free number. The phone call logs will be used to learn topics and questions of interest. 30 users will be randomly called back to ask for a follow up survey.
